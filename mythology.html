c. 2100 BCE

Epic of Gilgamesh

The oldest known piece of epic literature, this Mesopotamian tale follows King Gilgamesh of Uruk on his quest for immortality. After the death of his friend Enkidu, Gilgamesh seeks the secret of eternal life from Utnapishtim, the survivor of a great flood. The epic explores themes of friendship, mortality, and the human condition, influencing countless stories including the Biblical flood narrative.

c. 2000-1400 BCE

The Minotaur

In Greek mythology, the Minotaur was a creature with the head of a bull and the body of a man, confined in an elaborate labyrinth beneath the palace of King Minos of Crete. Born from the union of Minos's wife Pasiphaë and a sacred bull, the monster demanded regular sacrifices of Athenian youth until the hero Theseus navigated the labyrinth and slew the beast with the help of Minos's daughter Ariadne.

c. 8th Century BCE

The Odyssey

Homer's epic poem follows the Greek hero Odysseus on his perilous ten-year journey home after the Trojan War. He encounters mythical creatures including the Cyclops Polyphemus, the enchantress Circe, the Sirens, and the sea monsters Scylla and Charybdis. The tale emphasizes cunning, perseverance, and loyalty as Odysseus struggles to return to his faithful wife Penelope and son Telemachus.

c. 5th-6th Century CE

King Arthur & Excalibur

The legendary British king who wielded the magical sword Excalibur and led the Knights of the Round Table. According to legend, only the rightful king could pull the sword from the stone. Arthur established Camelot, a utopian kingdom, with the help of the wizard Merlin. His quest for the Holy Grail and the betrayal by Lancelot and Queen Guinevere led to his kingdom's downfall and his mysterious departure to Avalon.

c. 8th-11th Century CE

Beowulf

This Old English epic poem tells of the Scandinavian hero Beowulf who comes to the aid of King Hrothgar of Denmark. The kingdom is terrorized by Grendel, a monstrous creature who attacks the mead hall at night. Beowulf defeats Grendel and later his vengeful mother in her underwater lair. Fifty years later, as an aging king, Beowulf faces a dragon in his final battle, sacrificing himself to save his people.

c. 8th-13th Century CE

1001 Arabian Nights

A collection of Middle Eastern folk tales including famous stories like Aladdin, Ali Baba and the Forty Thieves, and Sinbad the Sailor. The frame story follows Scheherazade, who tells her husband, King Shahryar, captivating stories each night to delay her execution. Her tales feature genies, magic carpets, enchanted lamps, and fantastic adventures that have captivated audiences for over a millennium.

c. 12th-13th Century CE

The Holy Grail

The legendary cup or chalice used by Jesus Christ at the Last Supper, said to possess miraculous powers including healing and eternal youth. Medieval Arthurian legends made the quest for the Grail a central theme, with knights like Galahad, Percival, and Lancelot seeking this sacred relic. Various locations have claimed to house the true Grail, from Glastonbury Abbey to Valencia Cathedral, making it one of Christianity's most enduring mysteries.

16th Century CE

El Dorado

The legend of a lost city of gold in South America drove Spanish conquistadors and explorers to undertake dangerous expeditions into uncharted territories. Originally referring to a Muisca chieftain who covered himself in gold dust during rituals, the myth evolved into tales of an entire golden city. Expeditions by Francisco de Orellana, Gonzalo Pizarro, and Walter Raleigh all failed to find the fabled city, though the legend persists.

16th Century CE

Fountain of Youth

The mythical spring that allegedly restores youth to anyone who drinks or bathes in its waters. Spanish explorer Juan Ponce de León is famously associated with searching for the fountain during his 1513 expedition to Florida, though historians debate whether this was his actual goal. The legend draws from ancient stories of healing waters and reflects humanity's eternal desire to reverse aging and achieve immortality.

c. 360 BCE (Origin), Popularized 19th-20th Century

Atlantis

First described by the Greek philosopher Plato, Atlantis was a powerful and advanced island nation that sank into the ocean "in a single day and night of misfortune." Plato described it as existing 9,000 years before his time, with concentric rings of water and land. Modern searches have proposed locations from the Mediterranean to Antarctica, but most scholars believe Plato created Atlantis as an allegory about hubris and divine punishment rather than a real place.

20th Century CE

Bigfoot (Sasquatch)

Large, hairy, ape-like creature said to inhabit forests in the Pacific Northwest. Indigenous peoples told stories of wild men for centuries, but modern interest surged in 1958 after large footprints were discovered in California. The Patterson-Gimlin film of 1967 showed what appeared to be a large bipedal creature, though its authenticity remains disputed. Despite numerous expeditions and claimed sightings, no conclusive evidence has been found.

20th Century CE

Loch Ness Monster

Affectionately called "Nessie," this legendary creature allegedly inhabits Loch Ness in the Scottish Highlands. Though local folklore mentioned water beasts for centuries, modern interest began with a 1933 sighting report. The famous "Surgeon's Photograph" of 1934 (later revealed as a hoax) showed what appeared to be a long-necked creature. Despite scientific surveys using sonar and submarines, no evidence of a large creature has been confirmed, yet the legend continues to attract tourists and researchers.
